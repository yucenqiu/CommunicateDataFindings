FordGoBike (Communicate Data Findings Project)¶

Introduction¶

This dataset contains bike sharing information posted in the month of April by Bay Wheels, formerly known as FordGoBike. This data set contains approximately 239,000 individual entries of users posting their data regarding their bike rides.

Data Cleaning¶

  • Remove the 64 data entries that contain null values for 'start_station_id', 'start_station_name', 'end_station_id', and 'end_station_name'
  • Change all of the id columns into a uniform data type.
  • Change data type to datetime for columns 'start_time' and 'end_time'
  • 'user_type' can be changed into a categorical data type
  • More information can be extracted from the 'start_time' and 'end_time' columns. We can derive information about the specific months, weekdays, hours from this aggregated data.
  • Converting 'duration_sec' from seconds to minutes.

What is the structure of your dataset?¶

There are 239,111 entries regarding individual bike ride trip information. Most data described in the csv file are are numeric data. There are a few categorical data such as start_station_name, end_station_name, user_type, and bike_share_for_all_trip I extracted specific data and formed new columns out of the start_time and end_time columns.

These new columns include: start/end date, start/end hour, start/end day of the week, start/end month, start/end minute, and start/end date in the month

What is/are the main feature(s) of interest in your dataset?¶

I'm interested in how nominal data such as the duration length is distributed based on various categorical data such as day of the week, start/end hour and etc.

What features in the dataset do you think will help support your investigation into your feature(s) of interest?¶

Duration length is by far the most important column that will help me navigate my features of interest. What is important is perhaps exploring various relationships and distributions that other variables may have with the duration length.

Talk about some of the relationships you observed in this part of the investigation. How did the feature(s) of interest vary with other features in the dataset?¶

Something I've stated back in the univariate exploration was that it seems that most people have their bike rides during rush hours for the weekdays, but with the boxplot in the bivariate exploration I found that the medians of bike rides during the weekday is lower than that of the bike rides during the weekends.

Did you observe any interesting relationships between the other features (not the main feature(s) of interest)?¶

Something interesting I've noticed is that just because the trip duration might be high, it doesn't necessary translate to a high change in starting and ending latitude or longitude. A plausible explanation may be that there may be a large amount of leisure or recreational rides and that the purpose is not meant for traveling distance. Another explanation could be that an average user did cover a reasonable distance but the net distance traveled doesn't amount to much if someone keeps riding in circles.

So in the multivariate exploration I mainly focused on the breakdown of the distributions of bike usage the two user types during the week. By layering different variables on top of each other, I was able to break the data down to see not only which day were more popular between the groups but also which hour in the day is most appealing to each type of user.

Were there any interesting or surprising interactions between features?¶

I've noticed that while both user types share the commonality of riding bikes during rush hours of the workdays, a low percentage of the subscribers seem to continue riding during the weekends while customers seems to retain a high percentage during the weekends. We can conclude that subscribers usually use the bikes as a main source of transportation for work while customers are perhaps people who use bike as a secondary mean to get to work or people who bike around on their leisure time.
